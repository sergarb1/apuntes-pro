Active vs Passive

Who is doing the action? What is receiving it?

Active Voice

The chef cooks the meal.

Focus: The Chef (the doer)

➜

Passive Voice

The meal is cooked by the chef.

Focus: The Meal (the receiver)
The core difference: active focuses on the doer, passive on the receiver.

When to Use Passive Voice?

  • When the action is more important than the doer.
  • When the doer is unknown or unimportant.
  • In formal or scientific writing for objectivity.
  • To avoid mentioning who is responsible.

"The report was finished." (Emphasis on the report, not who finished it).

There are specific reasons to choose passive. Focus on the reason, not just the grammar rule.

When NOT to use Passive?

Avoid it if you want to be clear, direct, or emphasize the agent!

  • Clarity & Directness: Active is usually more concise.
  • Emphasize the Agent: If 'who' did it matters, use active.
  • Daily Communication: Active sounds more natural.
Overusing passive can make your writing sound boring or vague. Choose active when the doer is important.

The Basic Structure

Always remember these two key components:

TO BE (conjugated) + PAST PARTICIPLE

The verb "to be" changes according to the original tense.

This is the golden rule. No passive without TO BE and a past participle.

3 Simple Steps to Transform

  1. The Object of the active sentence becomes the Subject.
    Ex: They built the house. → The house...
  2. Add TO BE in the same tense as the active verb.
    Ex: They built the house (Past Simple). → The house was...
  3. Change the main verb to its Past Participle.
    Ex: They built the house. → The house was built.
Break it down into these three manageable steps for any transformation.

"by" vs "with": Agent or Instrument?

Don't confuse who did it with what was used!

  • "by" + AGENTE: Indicates who performed the action.
    Ex: The window was broken by the boy.
  • "with" + INSTRUMENT: Indicates the tool or object used.
    Ex: The window was broken with a hammer.
  • Together: The window was broken by the boy with a hammer.
A common point of confusion. Remember: 'by' for the person, 'with' for the tool.

Passive Voice Across Tenses (Part 1)

Tense Active Example Passive Example
Present Simple She writes letters. Letters are written.
Present Continuous She is writing letters. Letters are being written.
Present Perfect She has written letters. Letters have been written.
Past Simple She wrote letters. Letters were written.
Past Continuous She was writing letters. Letters were being written.

Tip: ¡El 'TO BE' es el que marca el tiempo! El Participio siempre es igual.

First part of the tenses table. Highlight that 'TO BE' takes the tense.

Passive Voice Across Tenses (Part 2)

Tense Active Example Passive Example
Past Perfect She had written letters. Letters had been written.
Future Simple (Will) She will write letters. Letters will be written.
Future Going To She is going to write letters. Letters are going to be written.
Modals (Can, Must, Should) She can write letters. Letters can be written.
Modals Perfect (Could Have) She could have written letters. Letters could have been written.

Consejo: ¡Con los Modales, siempre "be" + Participio! (Can be done, must be said...).

Second part of the tenses table, covering future and modals.

Advanced: Reporting Verbs

Report what "is said" or "is believed" without naming the source.

Structure 1: It is said/believed/known that...

Ex: It is believed that he is innocent.

Structure 2: Subject + is/are said/believed/known + to + Infinitive

Ex: He is believed to be innocent.

For past actions, use "to have + Past Participle".

Ex: He is said to have stolen the car.
These structures are very common in formal reports and news. They allow for an impersonal reporting style.

Advanced: Get Passive

A more informal and dynamic way to form the passive, often implying a change or event.

Formula: GET + PAST PARTICIPLE

Ex: I got paid today. (More colloquial than "I was paid"). Ex: They got caught stealing.

Often implies something unexpected or a process unfolding.

The 'get' passive is common in spoken English and can add a sense of action or process.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting the verb "TO BE". (e.g., "The car repaired yesterday.")
  • Using the wrong tense for "TO BE". (e.g., "The book was read" for "is read").
  • Not using the Past Participle form of the main verb.
  • Overusing the passive voice in informal contexts.
These are the most common pitfalls. Be mindful of them and practice to overcome them.
