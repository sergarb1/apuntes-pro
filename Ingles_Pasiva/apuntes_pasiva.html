The Passive Voice

Mastering focus and emphasis in English

What is the Passive Voice? (Explained simply!)

Imagine you have a really cool toy, let's say, a racing car. There are two main ways to talk about it:

  • Active Voice: "My brother built this car." Here, your brother is the "hero," the one who did the action. He's the focus.
  • Passive Voice: "This car was built by my brother." Now the car is the "hero"! The important thing is what happened to the car, not so much who built it.

In short, the passive voice shifts the focus from the person or thing doing the action (the agent) to the person or thing receiving the action (the object). It's like changing the camera angle in a movie. And it's super useful!

Quick Tip: The Zombie Test!
If you can add "by zombies" at the end of a sentence and it still makes sense, it's very likely passive! (e.g., "The car was stolen *by zombies*"). If not, it's probably active.

Why, when and where to use the Passive Voice?

The passive voice is a powerful tool in English to change the focus of a sentence. We don't use it just to "sound smarter," but because it allows us to be clearer or more diplomatic in certain situations. We use it when the object (the received action) is more important or relevant than the subject (the one performing the action), or when the agent is unknown, obvious, or we want to keep it in the background.

  • 1. Focus on the result/action: When what truly interests us is what happened, rather than who did it.
    Ex: "The bridge was built in 1890." (The construction of the bridge is important; the builder could be a team, a generic entity, and we don't need to name them to understand the fact).
  • 2. Unknown or obvious agent: When we don't know who performed the action, or it's so obvious it doesn't need to be stated.
    Ex: "My wallet was stolen." (I don't know who stole it, so it's more honest not to say "someone"). "The thief was arrested." (It's obvious it was the police, right? No need to say it).
  • 3. Formal or scientific texts: To maintain an objective and impersonal tone. This way, the text focuses on the facts, not on the people who observed or performed them.
    Ex: "The experiment was conducted at high temperatures." (Sounds more scientific than "We conducted the experiment...").
  • 4. Avoid mentioning the agent: Sometimes, out of courtesy, to avoid blaming someone, or simply because the agent is not relevant to the main message.
    Ex: "Mistakes were made." (Sounds softer than "You made mistakes.").

When NOT to use Passive Voice? (Less is more!)

Although useful, using the passive voice all the time can make your writing sound a bit boring, robotic, or difficult to understand. Think of it as a special tool; don't use it for everything. It's best to avoid it when:

  • 1. You want to be clear and direct: The active voice is almost always more concise and easier to understand. It's like getting straight to the point.
  • 2. You want to emphasize the agent: If it's important *who* did the action, put them first! The active voice is your best friend here.
  • 3. In most daily communication: We speak and write in active voice naturally. Excessive passive voice sounds forced.

The Magic Formula of the Passive (Memorize it!)

This is the fundamental structure of any sentence in the passive voice. It's like a recipe that always works!

OBJECT + verb TO BE (conjugated) + PAST PARTICIPLE + (by SUBJECT)
Recuerda: the verb "TO BE" is the one that adapts to the tense of the original active sentence. The PARTICIPLE NEVER changes its form.

Steps to transform an Active sentence to Passive (Like a detective!)

Let's convert an active sentence to passive step by step. It's like taking apart a clock and putting it back together!

1

Identify the Object of the active sentence and turn it into the Subject of the passive.

Ex: He writes books. → The object here is BOOKS! Now it's our new protagonist.
2

Look at the tense of the main verb in the active sentence and put the verb TO BE in that same tense.

Ex: He writes books. ("writes" is Present Simple). For "BOOKS" (plural) in Present Simple, we use ARE.
3

Transform the main verb of the active sentence into its Past Participle (the "3rd column" of irregular verbs).

Ex: He writes books. The participle of "write" is WRITTEN. ¡Y listo! Books are written.

"by" vs "with": Who does what and with what?

In the passive voice, we often mention the agent (who performs the action) or the instrument (what the action is performed with). It's important not to confuse them!

  • "by" + AGENT: Indicates who performs the action.
    Ej: The window was broken by the boy. (The boy is the agent who broke the window).
  • "with" + INSTRUMENT: Indicates the tool or object used to perform the action.
    Ej: The window was broken with a hammer. (The hammer is the instrument used).
  • Both together: The window was broken by the boy with a hammer.

Verb Tenses in Passive Voice (Your Magic Cheat Sheet!)

Here's how to adapt the passive voice to the most common verb tenses. Pay attention to how the form of the verb TO BE changes, because that's the key to the verb tense!

Tense Active Voice Example Passive Voice Example
Present Simple She writes books. Books are written by her.
Present Continuous She is writing books. Books are being written by her.
Present Perfect She has written books. Books have been written by her.
Past Simple She wrote books. Books were written by her.
Past Continuous She was writing books. Books were being written by her.
Past Perfect She had written books. Books had been written by her.
Future Simple (Will) She will write books. Books will be written by her.
Future Going To She is going to write books. Books are going to be written by her.
Modals (Can, Must, Should) She can write books. Books can be written by her.
Modals Perfect (Could Have) She could have written books. Books could have been written by her.

Passive for Experts: Beyond the Basics!

1. Passive with Reporting Verbs (Official Gossip!)

Sometimes, we want to report what "is said," "is believed," or "is expected," without saying who says it. It's very common in news or reports.

Structure 1: It is said/believed/known that...

Ex: It is believed that he is innocent. (Se cree que es inocente).

Structure 2: Subject + is/are said/believed/known + to + Infinitive

Ex: He is believed to be innocent. (Él es creído ser inocente).

¡Quick Tip! If we're talking about the past, "to" is followed by "have + participle".

Ex: He is said to have stolen the car. (Se dice que robó el coche).

2. The "GET" Passive (More Informal and Dynamic!)

Besides "TO BE," we can use "GET" to form the passive, especially in informal situations, to talk about something that happens to us or to emphasize a change.

Formula: GET + PAST PARTICIPLE

Ex: I got paid today. (More colloquial than "I was paid"). Ex: They got caught stealing.

It often implies something unexpected or a process.

Common Mistakes (Everyone makes them at first!)

  • 1. Forgetting "TO BE": The passive *always* needs it. Without it, it's like a car without an engine.
    Incorrect: "Books written by her." | Correct: "Books are written by her."
  • 2. Confusing "TO BE" tenses: If the active verb was "writes" (present), "TO BE" must be "is/are". If it was "wrote" (past), "TO BE" must be "was/were". It's the tense thermometer!
    Incorrect: "Books was written." | Correct: "Books were written."
  • 3. Using infinitive or gerund instead of participle: The main verb *always* goes in the past participle. No exceptions.
    Incorrect: "Books are writing." | Correct: "Books are written."
  • 4. Overusing the passive voice: Remind yourself "when NOT to use it". If active is clearer, use active!
